## Tempering: dark couverture seeding method

**Topic:** tempering  
**Tags:** dark_chocolate, seeding, valrhona, callebaut, couverture

**Content:**
For dark couverture chocolate (60-72% cocoa), the industry-standard seeding curve is: melt to 50-55°C (122-131°F), then add 5% by weight of pre-tempered pistoles (Callets, fèves, or chopped tempered chocolate) at ambient temperature (15-20°C). Stir continuously while the seed chocolate melts and cools the mass. Target working temperature is 31-32°C (88-90°F). The chocolate must be kept moving; standing chocolate develops type IV or V crystal instability. Digital laser thermometers are preferred over probe thermometers — probes disturb the crystal formation. Signs of correct temper: thin coating on a dipped knife sets glossy in 3-5 minutes at 20°C room temperature, with an audible snap when broken.

**Source:** Callebaut Chocolate Academy, "Tempering with Callets" (callebaut.com/en-US/chocolate-video/technique/tempering/callets); Valrhona L'École technical guide.

## Tempering: milk and white chocolate

**Topic:** tempering  
**Tags:** milk_chocolate, white_chocolate, ruby, seeding

**Content:**
Milk chocolate tempering curve: melt to 45-50°C (113-122°F), cool with seed to 27-28°C (80-82°F), work at 29-30°C (84-86°F). White and ruby chocolate: melt to 45°C (113°F), seed to 26-27°C (79-81°F), work at 28-29°C (82-84°F). The lower temperatures reflect milk solids' lower melt point and heat sensitivity — above 50°C, milk solids begin to burn and produce off-flavors. White chocolate is particularly sensitive; exceeding 50°C during melting irreversibly damages the flavor. Ruby chocolate, introduced by Barry Callebaut in 2017, behaves similarly to white in tempering but is more acid-sensitive.

**Source:** Callebaut technical data sheets; Valrhona Ruby RB1 product sheet.

## Ganache ratios and shelf life

**Topic:** ganache_ratios  
**Tags:** ganache, truffle, bonbon_filling, water_activity

**Content:**
Canonical chocolate-to-cream ratios by weight: 1:1 (soft filling for mother ganache or dipped truffle centers), 2:1 (firm piping ganache or rolled truffles), 1:2 (pourable glaze or fondue). Milk and white chocolate require higher chocolate ratios (2.5:1 or 3:1 for firm applications) because their lower cocoa solids and higher sugar/fat load produce softer structures. Unstabilized ganaches have water activity (aw) near 0.90, supporting bacterial growth and limiting refrigerated shelf life to 10-14 days. Standard 1:1 dark ganache has aw ~0.86 (3 weeks refrigerated). Stabilized ganaches (glucose syrup + invert sugar + sorbitol) can reach aw 0.75-0.85 and last 6-8 weeks. Water-based ganaches (fruit pulp) have aw >0.90 and must be treated as perishable with 5-7 day shelf life.

**Source:** Peter Greweling, Chocolates and Confections, 2nd ed., CIA; Ramon Morató, Chocolate: The Reference Standard.

## Water activity and microbial growth

**Topic:** water_activity  
**Tags:** aw, food_safety, shelf_life, haccp

**Content:**
Water activity (aw) is the free water available for microbial growth, ranging 0-1.0. Bacteria generally require aw >0.91 to grow; most spoilage yeasts require >0.88; most molds require >0.80. Shelf-stable confections target aw <0.75. For chocolatiers, lowering aw extends shelf life without refrigeration. Glucose syrup and invert sugar (Trimoline) lower aw through osmotic pressure. Sorbitol and glycerin bind water as humectants. Alcohol ≥40% ABV (spirits) adds direct antimicrobial effect. A typical stabilized bonbon ganache formula targets: chocolate 45%, cream 30%, glucose syrup 10%, invert sugar 5%, sorbitol 5%, butter 5% — yielding aw ~0.80 and 6-8 week shelf life.

**Source:** UC Davis Master Food Preserver, "Water Activity and Food Preservation" (ucanr.edu/program/uc-master-food-preserver-program/article/water-activity-and-its-role-food-preservation); Peter Greweling, Chocolates and Confections, ch. 5.

## FDA Big-9 allergens

**Topic:** allergens  
**Tags:** fda, faster_act, labeling, big_9

**Content:**
The FDA's nine major food allergens as of January 1, 2023 (FASTER Act 2021 added sesame): milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, sesame. Derived ingredients trigger the same labeling: butter/cream/whey/ghee → milk; brioche/panini bread/most pastry → wheat; sesame oil/tahini/halva/everything bagel seasoning → sesame. Soy lecithin in chocolate triggers soy labeling unless the manufacturer holds the FDA's narrow exemption; conservative default is to flag soy. Tree nuts include almond, walnut, pecan, hazelnut, cashew, pistachio, macadamia, brazil, pine, filbert. Cross-contact (shared equipment, shared kitchen surfaces, shared oil) must be disclosed when the hazard is known — FDA's "may contain" labeling.

**Source:** FDA, "Food Allergies" (fda.gov/food/nutrition-food-labeling-and-critical-foods/food-allergies); FASTER Act of 2021.

## HACCP temperatures and cooling

**Topic:** haccp  
**Tags:** haccp, fda_food_code, danger_zone, cooling

**Content:**
The FDA Food Code temperature danger zone is 41-135°F (5-57°C). Cold foods must be held ≤41°F; hot foods ≥135°F. Cooling rule for cooked foods: 135°F to 70°F within 2 hours, then 70°F to 41°F within 4 additional hours (total 6 hours). Time outside of refrigeration during preparation counts against a 4-hour total exposure. Finished chocolate storage: 65-68°F at 50-60% relative humidity. Higher temperatures cause fat bloom (triacylglycerol migration to surface). Higher humidity causes sugar bloom (condensation-driven sugar recrystallization). Both are safety-neutral but quality-destructive.

**Source:** FDA Food Code 2022; USDA FSIS Guide to Time/Temperature Control (fsis.usda.gov); Lake Champlain Chocolates, "What is Chocolate Bloom?"

## Bonbon structure and shell-mold production

**Topic:** enrobing  
**Tags:** bonbon, mold, shell, polycarbonate, airbrush

**Content:**
A molded bonbon consists of three components: decoration (optional, applied before shell), shell (tempered chocolate coating the mold cavity), and filling (ganache, praline, caramel, pâte de fruit). Standard production: decorate cold polycarbonate mold with tinted cocoa butter (airbrushed at 32-35°C, splattered, finger-painted, sponged, or transfer-sheet applied); fill cavities with tempered chocolate; invert mold to drain shell, scrape, set at 15-18°C; deposit filling leaving 2-3mm space at top; set filling; cap with tempered chocolate; scrape; cool and demold. A 21-cavity polycarbonate mold yields ~240-260g finished bonbons at ~11-12g per piece (shell ~5g, filling ~6g, cap ~1g). Decoration colors are made by mixing tempered cocoa butter with fat-soluble pigments; water-based colors bloom the chocolate.

**Source:** Ecole Chocolat, "The Making of a Bonbon"; Peter Greweling, Chocolates and Confections, ch. 11; Callebaut Chocolate Academy mold techniques.

## Truffle structure and rolling/enrobing methods

**Topic:** enrobing  
**Tags:** truffle, ganache, rolled, dipped, cocoa_powder

**Content:**
Truffle production methods, in order of complexity: (1) rolled — pipe 2:1 ganache with a #4 round tip onto parchment, let crystallize at 18-20°C for 2 hours, hand-roll into spheres, pre-coat by dipping bare hands in tempered chocolate and rolling, finish by rolling in cocoa powder or chopped nuts; (2) dipped — pipe ganache centers, crystallize, pre-coat, dip with a round dipping fork in tempered chocolate, place on parchment, optionally decorate with filigree from a paper cone; (3) slab-cut — spread ganache 1cm thick in a guitar frame or hotel pan, let crystallize 24 hours at 18°C, cut into squares or diamonds, pre-coat, dip, finish. Standard yield for rolled truffles: 500g ganache → ~45-50 truffles at 10-12g each. Standard yield for dipped truffles: 400g ganache + 200g tempered dipping chocolate → ~40 finished truffles at 14-16g each.

**Source:** Peter Greweling, Chocolates and Confections, 2nd ed., "Rolled and Piped Truffles" and "Slabbed Ganache"; Jean-Pierre Wybauw, Fine Chocolates: Great Experience.
